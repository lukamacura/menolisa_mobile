# 1. MARKET POSITIONING

## The Positioning Gap

The menopause app market has a structural problem that works in your favor: every competitor has positioned themselves as a healthcare tool. They lead with clinical credibility, medical advisory boards, and symptom management frameworks. This is rational—but it misses what women actually say they want when they talk about menopause.

Across thousands of posts in menopause communities, review sites, and social media, the dominant emotional themes are not “I need better symptom tracking.” They are:

•       “I feel like I’m going crazy and nobody understands.”

•       “My doctor spent five minutes with me and told me it’s just part of aging.”

•       “I don’t even know if what I’m experiencing is menopause or something else.”

•       “I feel completely alone in this.”

The #1 unmet need is not information. It is companionship and validation. Someone (or something) that is available when they need it, that actually remembers what they’ve shared, that connects dots they can’t see, and that never makes them feel dismissed.

## Your Competitive Positioning Statement

|   |
|---|
|_MenoLisa is the first AI menopause companion—a knowledgeable friend who’s available 24/7, never dismisses what you’re feeling, actually remembers your history, and helps you see patterns in your symptoms that your doctor doesn’t have time to find._|

## The Unique Mechanism: Pattern Intelligence™

Every successful direct response offer needs a unique mechanism—a proprietary system or process that explains why THIS solution works when everything else the prospect has tried has failed. For MenoLisa, the mechanism is what the AI does beyond simple tracking.

**Name it:** Lisa’s Pattern Intelligence™

**What it does:** Lisa cross-references your symptom timing, severity, triggers, and lifestyle patterns to reveal connections that are invisible to you and that your doctor doesn’t have time to find. The longer you use Lisa, the smarter she gets about YOUR body.

**Why it’s different:** Other apps let you log symptoms into a void. Lisa reads your data, finds the patterns, and tells you what she’s noticed—then drafts shareable notes you can bring to your doctor.

The “What Lisa Noticed” feature in your app IS the mechanism. It just needs to be named, branded, and positioned as the core differentiator in all marketing.

## The Origin Story

This story should be used everywhere: the app store listing, website About page, social media bios, press inquiries, and the first 30 seconds of any video content. It is the emotional anchor of the entire brand.

|   |
|---|
|_“When I started going through menopause, I felt completely lost. My doctor spent five minutes with me and told me it was ‘just part of aging.’ I’d be up at 2 AM with night sweats and anxiety, Googling whether what I was experiencing was normal. I couldn’t find a single thing that felt like it actually understood what I was going through._<br><br>_My nephew is a software developer, and one day I told him: ‘Why isn’t there something that just GETS what I’m going through and helps me make sense of it?’ So we built Lisa together—the menopause companion I wished I’d had from day one.”_|

## Public Roles

**Your aunt (name TBD):** Co-founder and face of MenoLisa. She is a real woman going through menopause who built this for herself and women like her. She creates all front-facing content: social media videos, community engagement, email newsletters, and public appearances. Her tone is warm, honest, sometimes funny, sometimes frustrated, and completely real.

**You (technical co-founder):** The builder. You stay behind the scenes for now. Your public role is limited to “behind the scenes” content (building the app, technical updates) and supporting your aunt’s content creation. You may surface in occasional content, but the emotional connection flows through her.

## Brand Voice Guidelines

•       Warm, conversational, never clinical or corporate

•       Uses “you” language, not “our users” or “patients”

•       Acknowledges frustration and validates feelings before offering solutions

•       Humor is welcome but never about the symptoms themselves—about the absurdity of how menopause is treated

•       Every piece of content should make a woman feel: “Finally, someone gets it.”

## Messaging Hierarchy

Every channel and asset should draw from these messages in priority order:

1.    Primary: “Lisa is the menopause companion who understands what you’re going through—available 24/7, never dismisses you.”

2.    Secondary: “Lisa’s Pattern Intelligence connects the dots in your symptoms that your doctor doesn’t have time to find.”

3.    Tertiary: “Built by a woman in menopause and her software-developer nephew—because she deserved better than ‘it’s just part of aging.’”

4.    Proof: “Take the free Menopause Quality of Life Assessment and see where you stand in 2 minutes.”

## Trial-to-Paid Conversion Strategy

The 3-day trial is short—and that’s both a risk and an advantage. The risk: less time to demonstrate value. The advantage: urgency is built in, and users who convert in 3 days have higher intent and lower churn than those who dawdle through a 7-day trial. But it means every hour of the trial must be engineered. There is zero room for a passive onboarding. Lisa must deliver her “wow” moment by Day 2 at the latest.

**Trial Day Sequence**

**Day 1 (Hour 0–6):** Immediate activation. The moment the user opens the app, Lisa greets them by name (from quiz data), references their top symptoms, and guides them through their first symptom log. Within the first session, Lisa should surface a quick observation based on quiz data: “Based on what you told me, your combination of night sweats and afternoon brain fog is a pattern I see often—let’s track it together.” Goal: make Lisa feel instantly personal and valuable. Push notification that evening: “How was your day? Log your symptoms before bed so I can start spotting patterns.”

**Day 2 (The Critical Day):** Pattern Intelligence preview. After 2–3 symptom logs, Lisa delivers her first “What Lisa Noticed” insight—even a preliminary one. This is THE conversion event. It must happen on Day 2, not later. Lisa also introduces the doctor note feature: “I can put together a summary you can show your doctor. Want me to start one?” Push notification: “Lisa noticed something about your symptoms. Open the app to see.”

**Day 3 (Conversion Day):** Morning push: “Your free access ends tonight. Lisa has started learning your patterns—keep the insights coming.” The paywall message is warm, not aggressive: “I’m just getting to know your body’s patterns. Stay with me and I’ll keep connecting the dots.” Show a preview of what’s coming: “By Week 2, Lisa typically identifies 3–5 patterns most women miss.”

## Value Equation Analysis (Hormozi Framework)

Dream Outcome: Feel understood, in control of menopause symptoms, and prepared for doctor visits. Perceived Likelihood: High—they’ve already experienced Lisa’s value in trial. Time Delay: Near-instant—Lisa responds in seconds, first insight within 48 hours. Effort & Sacrifice: Minimal—talking to Lisa is easier than journaling, tracking spreadsheets, or booking appointments. The 3-day trial creates natural urgency: users activate faster and experience value sooner than with longer trials.

At $12/month, this is less than one copay, one therapy session, or one bottle of supplements. The price-to-value ratio should feel like a no-brainer.

  

# 4. THE QUIZ FUNNEL (Highest-Leverage Asset)

Your onboarding quiz is currently locked inside the app download. This is your single biggest missed opportunity. Here’s why and how to fix it.

## Why the Quiz Is Your #1 Marketing Asset

Women LOVE assessments about their health. “What’s your menopause quality of life score?” is inherently shareable and curiosity-driven. It gives you email capture BEFORE asking for an app download. It provides a personalized result that creates emotional investment. And it generates data you can use for segmented marketing.

## The Standalone Quiz Funnel

**Step 1: Entry Point**

A dedicated landing page at menolisa.com/quiz (or similar). All social content, group posts, and eventually paid ads drive here. Headline: “What’s Your Menopause Score? Take the Free 2-Minute Assessment.”

**Step 2: Quiz Flow**

Use the same 8-step quiz from the app, but adapted for web. Each step should feel personal and validating. After the breather step, add copy: “You’re not imagining this. Let’s see what your experience tells us.”

**Step 3: Email Gate**

After the quiz is complete but BEFORE showing results: “Your personalized Menopause Quality of Life Score is ready. Enter your email to see your results.” This is where you capture the lead.

**Step 4: Results Page**

Show the quality-of-life score, severity breakdown, and personalized copy. This page should make the woman feel seen and validated. Below the results: “Want Lisa to track your patterns and help you make sense of what’s happening? Download the app—your profile is already started.”

**Step 5: Follow-Up Sequence**

Those who don’t download immediately enter a 5-email nurture sequence (detailed in Section 6).

|   |
|---|
|**TECHNICAL PRIORITY**<br><br>Building this standalone quiz page is the single most important technical task before launch. It is more important than any app feature. Every marketing channel you build will drive to this quiz. Without it, you’re asking cold traffic to download an app from an unknown brand—which has a conversion rate near zero. With it, you’re asking them to take a free quiz—which converts 20–40% of visitors to email leads.|

  

# 5. DISTRIBUTION STRATEGY

You have zero audience. That means the first 90 days are about building one—fast, for free or nearly free, in places where menopausal women already congregate.

## Channel 1: Facebook Menopause Groups (Primary)

This is your #1 channel. There are Facebook groups with 50,000 to 200,000+ members dedicated to menopause support. These women are posting daily about symptoms, asking questions, and actively seeking solutions. Your aunt needs to become a genuine, active member.

**The 30-Day Group Infiltration Strategy**

**Week 1–2 (pure value, zero promotion):** Your aunt joins 5–10 of the largest menopause groups. She posts about her own experience, answers other women’s questions, reacts to posts, and builds her reputation as a helpful community member. She does NOT mention MenoLisa. She is simply a woman going through menopause who is active and supportive.

**Week 3 (soft introduction):** In response to relevant posts (“I wish I could see patterns in my symptoms” or “I need something to show my doctor”), she mentions that she’s been working on something: “My nephew and I actually built an app for this—I was so frustrated with what was out there that we made our own. It’s been helping me see connections I never noticed.” This is conversational, not promotional.

**Week 4+ (quiz sharing):** Share the quiz as a resource: “Hey everyone, we made a free menopause quality of life assessment—no download needed, takes 2 minutes. I found my score really eye-opening. [link]” Many groups allow resource sharing if the member is established and the resource is genuinely helpful.

## Channel 2: TikTok & Instagram Reels (Brand Awareness)

Women aged 40–60 are the fastest-growing demographic on TikTok. Raw, authentic menopause content performs extremely well because it’s taboo-adjacent—people are curious but nobody talks about it openly.

**Content Pillars (Your Aunt Creates 3–5 Videos Per Week)**

1.    “Things Nobody Warned Me About” — Raw, relatable content about unexpected symptoms (brain fog, rage, anxiety). These get shared because women relate.

2.    “What I Tracked This Week” — Screen recordings or verbal summaries of what Lisa helped her discover. This is product demonstration disguised as personal storytelling.

3.    “Doctor Visit Prep” — Show the doctor note feature in action. “I showed my doctor THIS and she actually listened.” This addresses a deep pain point.

4.    “2 AM Menopause Club” — Late-night, can’t-sleep content. Incredibly relatable. “Who else is up right now?” Creates community feeling.

5.    “Quiz Reaction” — “I took my menopause quality of life score and here’s what I got.” CTA: link in bio to take the quiz.

## Channel 3: Pinterest (Long-Term SEO)

Create pins around menopause symptoms, tips, and lifestyle content. Link to blog posts or the quiz. Pinterest content has a long shelf life—pins from 6 months ago still drive traffic. This is a set-and-compound channel. Invest 2–3 hours per week creating 10–15 pins.

## Channel 4: Email Nurture (Conversion Engine)

Everyone who takes the quiz but doesn’t download the app enters a 5-email sequence over 10 days. This is where you convert quiz-takers into app users.

**Email 1 (immediate):** “Your Menopause Score Results + What They Mean.” Recap their score with personalized insight. Soft CTA to download.

**Email 2 (day 2):** “The Pattern Most Women Miss.” Educational content about symptom connections. Positions Lisa’s Pattern Intelligence as the solution. Medium CTA.

**Email 3 (day 4):** “What [Aunt’s Name] Learned in Her First Week with Lisa.” Personal story from your aunt about how tracking revealed a pattern. Social proof.

**Email 4 (day 7):** “What to Tell Your Doctor (and How Lisa Helps).” Focuses on the doctor note feature. Practical value.

**Email 5 (day 10):** “Ready to Meet Lisa?” Direct CTA. Include a limited-time extended trial (5 days instead of 3) for email subscribers as urgency.
